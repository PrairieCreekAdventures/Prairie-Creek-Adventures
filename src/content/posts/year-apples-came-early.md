The first apple dropped on August 29th. I heard it from the porch — that particular soft thud of a fruit hitting packed earth — and knew, before I even walked out to look, that we were in for an early harvest.

By the second week of September, the Galas were nearly done. The Honeycrisps, which usually hold until early October, were already softening on the branch. We picked what we could, pressed more cider than we had jars for, and gave buckets away to the neighbors.

The birds understood before we did. The cedar waxwings came in a cloud one morning and stripped a whole row of Winesaps in a single afternoon. Henry watched them from the barn window and said only, "Well. They needed them more."

## What we learned

- Warm winters shift everything. The buds broke two weeks early. Every stage after that compressed.
- Cider made from early-picked fruit tastes brighter, but it does not keep. Drink it within the month.
- The bees were fine. The orchard was fine. It was the timing, not the harvest, that changed.

Nothing about farming is ever the same twice. That is the hardest and best part.
